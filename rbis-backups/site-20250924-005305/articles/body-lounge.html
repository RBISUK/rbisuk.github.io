Body Lounge: Reading Micro-Expressions in a Digital World

Warm people. Serious work. This is how we translate tiny human signals into defensible, bias-aware notes.

Last updated: • Author: RBIS Intelligence

Scope. We analyse language, timing, and interaction patterns in digital comms (email, chat, forms, transcripts). We don’t infer protected characteristics or intent that the evidence can’t support.

Guardrails. No psychoanalysis. Notes must be observable (what we saw), verifiable (someone else can check), and proportional (relevant to the question).

1) Signals that travel well online

  • Timing windows. Consistent rapid replies to one party, slow to another → potential priority bias.
  • Contradictory anchors. Two adjacent statements with incompatible specifics (dates, amounts, “never” vs “yesterday”).
  • Escalation markers. Sudden switch to caps/ultimatums after a reasonable prompt → frustration trend, not credibility by itself.
  • Attachment lineage. File names and versioning patterns that don’t line up with the story told.

2) Bias traps (and how we avoid them)

  • Confidence ≠ correctness. Decisive tone can still be factually wrong.
  • Formality ≠ credibility. Polished writing is a style, not proof.
  • Emotion ≠ manipulation. Distress can reflect lived conditions.

Our rule: if it’s not in the evidence, it’s not in the finding.

3) Our note-taking method (R.E.A.D.)

  1. Record: quote the minimum text needed; timestamp; link the source.
  2. Exhibit: pin into a numbered bundle; keep a breadcrumb.
  3. Align: map to the question asked (scope control).
  4. Draft: one-line “why this matters” in plain English.

5) How we write it up

Findings use neutral verbs (“states”, “provides”, “omits”), avoid certainty beyond evidence, and include caveats where material is missing.

Template: One-liner finding

“Between 1–12 May, Party A did not provide requested repair photos despite three prompts (Refs: E12, E15, E18). This limits assessment of current condition.”

6) Limits

  • No medical, psychological, or legal opinions.
  • No inference of protected traits or private beliefs.
  • We highlight uncertainty explicitly.
